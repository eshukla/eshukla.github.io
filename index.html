Research

Here's more about my research.

I have a broad area of research interests ranging from gravitational physics, numerical relativity, analytical relativity, alternative theories of gravity and computational cosmology. Based on the research projects that I have worked on so far, I have developed a keen interest on few specific topics such as non-linearities in merger-ringdown phase of CBC, dynamical horizons, black hole mimickers etc. One problem that I am particularly interested is the study non-linearities during the highly dynamical and non-linear regime of binary black hole mergers by using the dynamical horizon formalism. This would help us to understand the formation of black holes, which brings me to my next main research interest. I wish to understand black hole's formation in greater depth which includes formation of singularities, critical phenomena during gravitational collapse etc. I am also interested in gravitational wave physics as a window to early universe cosmology such as stochastic background, primordial black holes, and early universe cosmological models (especially using numerical relativity). In general, I wish to do research on areas that strike the right balance between computational and theoretical techniques.

Research Experience

Visiting Student Researcher

August 2021 - Present

ICTS Bangalore

I have been selected for the Long Term Visiting Students Programme (LTVSP) 2021. I am working on dynamical horizons. The notion of event horizons is too global and teleological in nature. For instance, one requires to describe the future null infinity to locate an event horizon. This makes event horizons more difficult to deal with when it comes to numerical relativity. Researchers have come up with a quasi-local horizon formalism called the dynamical horizons that can be defined locally and can be used to study the physics of black hole formation during the mergers. In this formalism, the dynamical horizon geometry is used to define mass and spin multipole moments of the given black hole. The multipole moments can be correlated with the properties of gravitational waves that we detect and subsequently these parameters can be used to study the mysterious horizon formation. Presently, I am learning to use numerical relativity (SpEC code) to simulate black hole mergers. Once we have the data from these simulations, we will extract dynamical horizons and the related multipole moments to study the underlying physics.

Supervised by- Dr Prayush Kumar

Research Project

October 2020 - Present

Johns Hopkins University

Black holes merger is a highly non-linear event which settles down to a perturbed Kerr BH during ringdown. The ringdown regime is so far seen to be a linear sum of quasinormal modes. This poses a pertinent question- where do these non-linearities from merger phase go? The answer to this question would provide better insight into the no-hair theorem, formation of black holes and more accurate methods to model gravitational waveforms. I am looking for these non-linearities such as second order coupling of quasinormal modes in the merger-ringdown phase of black hole mergers. The work closely builds on the recently published paper by Giesler et al . The draft is currently under preparation and we will soon be publishing our findings.

Supervised by- Prof. Emanuele Berti
